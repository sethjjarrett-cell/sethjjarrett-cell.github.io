Region 01  /  First stop

North
America

New York, Washington, Miami. A family trip -- mum, sister, brother -- and a first time in the States. The scale of the country, the disparity, and losing a bank card in Washington: by the time I noticed, someone had spent two thousand pounds in Target. And ordered Uber Eats. That one did not get refunded; I suppose it was on me.

New York Washington DC Miami

Stop 01

New York

First stop, and a family trip to the big apple. New York is grand in a way you have to physically see. What struck me most was the disparity: serious wealth and serious poverty side by side, homelessness on a level you do not see in London. One morning we walked ten minutes to a diner, a couple of blocks in central Manhattan, and I counted three people shooting heroin and one smoking crack on the way. Twenty-five years in the UK, a year of it in London, and I had never seen either.

The Empire State Building -- went up for the view. The city from above changes how you understand it at street level. We caught it at sunset and stayed into the dark, watching people in office blocks and apartments wind down from another big day.
Central Park -- enormous, and a proper park: distinct sections, baseball games going in several places at once. Spent a good stretch of a day in it. I understand why it is so iconic.
Natural History Museum -- one of the better museums I have been to. Also the setting of Night at the Museum, a family favourite, which made it a required stop.
Brooklyn Bridge and Brooklyn -- walked across and into the start of Brooklyn, people skating in the square, a part of the city that knows it is cool. The view back across the water to Manhattan is one of the best city views I have seen, and I do not understand why it is not more famous. Also where I accepted that British bridges are pathetic next to their American, and even more so Chinese, counterparts.

Stop 02

Washington DC

After New York: Arlington Cemetery, the monuments, the tourist circuit. A very considered, grand city, and it feels purpose-built because it is -- the height limits keep long views open to the federal buildings and monuments. Also where I lost my bank card. Someone found it and spent about two thousand pounds in Target within the hour, in a rich capital city. It says something about the States: capitalism breeds an individualist mindset, and people who are struggling can end up treating society as a zero-sum game.

Arlington Cemetery -- rows of identical white headstones as far as you can see. Genuinely quite moving. The Americans do dramatic well.
The monuments and the Mall -- the White House, the Capitol, the Lincoln Memorial, the Washington Monument. Grand civic buildings on a grand civic scale, plus the churches and sights around them. A city that takes its own importance seriously.
Lost the bank card -- dropped it somewhere, and someone ran up about two thousand pounds in Target before I had noticed. Plus an Uber Eats order, which did not get refunded. I suppose that one was on me.

Stop 03

Miami

Very hot, very multicultural, a particular energy to it -- chains, Spanish music, Cuban influence all over the place. People say Miami is basically not America, and I can see why. Miami Beach was a bit oversaturated and expensive; Wynwood was much better.

Miami Beach -- very hot, very expensive, slightly oversaturated. The Cuban and Latin influence gives it an energy that is specific to that city.
Wynwood -- a real place compared to Miami Beach: cool clothes stores, murals, a neighbourhood with actual character. An American-Ecuadorian friend took me, and it would not have occurred to me to wander into the city otherwise.
Out of my depth -- truthfully, at times. The American style, particularly the tougher Floridian version of it, is a bit intimidating to a Brit.

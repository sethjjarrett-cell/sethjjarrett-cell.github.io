§1.1

Wind–Solar Mini-Grid — Feasibility Study

Individual · 83% · Cohort exemplar

Abstract. An independent, end-to-end feasibility study of a hybrid wind–solar mini-grid, assessed across four load scenarios. The work covers resource modelling, storage optimisation, and full lifetime cost analysis, and was later used as an exemplar paper for the following cohort.

  • Wind resource modelled with Weibull distributions to estimate generation across the candidate site
  • Battery depth-of-discharge and lifecycle optimisation evaluated across all four load scenarios
  • Full cost analysis: capital expenditure, operation & maintenance, and payback period per scenario
Worked example When the measurement plan fails: wind data without the wind ▸

The grid was for a Christmas tree farm near Bristol, built around an old American-style wind pump being converted into a turbine. The plan for the wind resource was empirical: fit a Weibull distribution to the local weather-station data, install an anemometer on the farm, and use the difference between the two to shift the distribution to what the turbine, surrounded by trees and far lower than the station’s mast, would actually see.

The plan failed in the field. During the measurement window the wind simply did not show up: most readings fell below the anemometer’s 0.7 m/s threshold and registered as zero, leaving no usable correction factor and no time to rerun the campaign. The replacement was analytical: the wind shear power law, V = V₁₀(H/10)1/α, with the exponent set by the ground surface, which for that site’s geography reduced the station data by 12% at the wind pump’s height. From there, the kinetic energy passing through the blades’ swept area (an annulus, since the blades start well clear of the hub) gave an estimated power curve, which was matched to a commercial turbine with a similar characteristic and interpolated at every time-stamped wind speed in the dataset.

The report is explicit that this is the study’s main limitation: an assumption-built power curve is not the same thing as measured performance. The exercise became less about finding a perfect answer and more about making defensible engineering assumptions with incomplete information, which, in practice, is most of the job.

The gap between the two power figures is the point. Output climbs steeply with speed, so the gusty hours above the mean earn more than the calm hours below it lose; reading the curve once at the mean speed always undersells the site. Solar DC Microgrid — Design & Build

Group industrial project · 40 credits

Abstract. Co-designed and helped build a solar DC microgrid for a remote off-grid community in the Brazilian Amazon — the full lifecycle from concept through simulation to validated hardware.

  • Owned the battery side: selection, the temperature and depth-of-discharge lifecycle analysis, and wire sizing
  • MATLAB algorithms sized the battery banks and array over five years of real irradiance data; PV panel selection was shared with a project partner
  • The power electronics, droop control, bidirectional battery-management converter and the validated PCB prototype were built by my three teammates
Worked example How long does a battery actually last, and what is that worth? ▸

The battery question was my section, and it is mostly an economics question wearing electrical clothes. Batteries are the most expensive part of a microgrid, so the design lives or dies on two things: how much usable capacity the site’s climate leaves you, and how hard you can cycle the battery before the lifetime maths turns against you.

The first is temperature. Capacity falls away sharply in the cold and plateaus once it is warm; reading the datasheet curve at the Amazon site’s 28°C average actually puts capacity slightly above nominal, at 100.2%. That is a rare case where the environment helps rather than hurts, and it means no derating penalty had to be carried through the rest of the sizing.

The second is depth of discharge, and it is the more interesting trade, because the two marked numbers answer different questions. Cycling shallow makes a battery last far longer, so the cumulative energy it delivers over its whole life (folding the cycle-life curve into the rated capacity and the temperature correction) peaks around a 53% depth of discharge, not at 100%. But that peak is the optimum for a narrow metric: energy per pound over decades of replacements. It was not the question the community’s economics were asking. Sizing to 53% would have made the bank roughly half as large again, spending scarce capital today to buy a benefit that only accrues across battery lifetimes. So the 80% is not an operating point at all; it is the design maximum, the deepest cycle the sizing must tolerate in the worst season. The full simulation bore that out: sized for the rainy months, the bank’s average DOD came out near 20%, nowhere near either marked figure. Recognising which question the peak answers, and that it was the wrong one, was the part I was most pleased with.

Calc note Busbar pairing as a combinatorics problem ▸

Each row in a data hall is fed by a pair of overhead busbar strings, each loaded to 50% so that either can carry the full row if the other fails. The strings run down the sides of the hall, fed from a ring at one end or the other: A–D from the north end, E–H from the south. The question is which pair feeds which row, and where on the hall each pair lands.

Treat it as combinatorics. Take an illustrative hall: eight strings (A–H, four per side) feeding sixteen rows. The number of distinct ways to pair two strings is the binomial coefficient,

⁸C₂ = 8! / (2! · 6!) = 28

so there are 28 unique pairs but only sixteen rows to fill. The job is to select sixteen of those pairs, not enumerate all of them. That is trivial to write out for eight strings, but the count explodes with string number, so in practice an algorithm generates the set and does the choosing. The selection balances two goals. First, even utilisation: across the sixteen rows each string should appear roughly the same number of times, or some strings sit idle while others carry the building. Second, side-mixing: a pair taken from a single ring loses both halves of that row’s supposedly redundant supply when that ring drops, whereas a cross-hall pair survives the loss of an entire ring.

Fig. A · illustrative hall (plan). Four string busbars run down each side: A–D fed from the north end, E–H from the south. Each live row carries two busbars, one tapped from a string on each side, and each row uses a different pair (here A+F, C+E, B+H). The rows before and after are drawn without busways to set the layout; the rest of the pair set feeds them.

Fig. B · the same hall with the ring mains drawn in, north ring lost. R1 paired one string from each side, so the south feed keeps it alive; R2 paired two strings from the same side, so both of its busbars die with the one ring. Same busbar count, opposite outcome, which is why the selection prefers cross-hall pairs.

Those two goals usually compete, and the weighting depends on what the client is optimising for. In this small example they barely compete at all: there are exactly sixteen cross-hall pairs for sixteen rows, so the balanced, fully cross-hall set is essentially forced. On a real hall, with more strings than rows or uneven sides, there are many balanced cross-hall sets, and the algorithm chooses among them, weighting in anything else that matters: keeping adjacent strings together, spreading load across switchgear, leaving room to grow.

Then comes a third lever the earlier sketch hides. The two rings sit at opposite ends of the hall: the north ring feeds A–D, the south ring feeds E–H. A north string only has to run as far south as its deepest row, and a south string only as far north as its shallowest. So once the pairs are fixed, where you place them on the rows decides how much busbar you install. Group a string’s rows toward its own end and the run terminates early; scatter them and it runs most of the length of the hall. The two ends pull against each other, since every row needs one feed from each, so you cannot shorten both at once. The algorithm sums the total run for every placement and keeps the cheapest. The saving matters more than it might look, because each of these busbars is rated to carry its entire string, so it is physically large, expensive kit rather than cable. On the illustrative hall an optimised placement comes out roughly 10–15% shorter than an arbitrary one, and when a single hall runs to around 20 MW and a campus can hold twenty or more of them, that percentage stops being academic.

Fig. C · the same eight cross-hall pairs (A–E, A–F, B–E, B–F, C–G, C–H, D–G, D–H) placed on eight rows two ways; dots mark the rows each string feeds. North strings run from the top feed down to their deepest row, south strings rise from the bottom to their shallowest. Left, the optimised placement keeps each string near its own end: 42 units of busbar. Right, the scattered placement makes every string run deep: 52 units, about a fifth more, for the identical pairs and loads.

What I built generates the pair set for any string count, makes the balanced cross-hall selection, then places it to minimise total run. The practical value was speed under change. When the string count or rack rating moved, and on a live job it moves, the whole assignment regenerated in seconds rather than being re-derived by hand.

How a preliminary design proceeds

Written as a runbook to be followed rather than an essay to be admired: the sequence of a utility-scale preliminary design in the order the constraints actually bite, with the checks that keep the numbers honest. Condensed below; the full version, with the worked P50/P90 example and the collected rules of thumb, is in the PDF.

Runbook Ten steps, in the order they bite ▸
  1. Requirements and constraints, before any software. Client requirements, land boundary, and above all what the project is optimising: yield per kWp, MWh per hectare, or cost per MWh. The grid connection belongs here too, since the export capacity at the point of interconnection fixes the AC size of the plant before a module is chosen.
  2. Resource, with stated suspicion. At least two meteo sources, the spread recorded, the best-validated one chosen and the reason written down. Temperature extremes, albedo and the soiling environment come along with it.
  3. The technology fork. Fixed tilt or tracker, module class and availability, central or string inverters. These reshape everything downstream, so they are decided explicitly even when the client has pre-decided.
  4. First pass in PVsyst. A deliberately simple baseline: tilt from latitude (with a 5 to 10 degree floor near the equator so rain cleans the modules), pitch from the shading limit, strings from the cold Voc and hot Vmp checks.
  5. Layout on the real site. Boundary and topography into PVcase, then the keep-outs that redraw layouts late if missed early: drainage, flood zones, wayleaves, buffers. Roads, fence, and the substation near the cable-length centroid. The buildable capacity emerges here.
  6. Electrical blocking. The plant is repeated power blocks: inverters per transformer skid, blocks per MV feeder, feeders plus spares setting the switchgear ways at the substation. Round numbers and symmetric blocks at every level.
  7. The optimisation matrix. Pitch, tilt and DC/AC swept as a grid across several inverter counts, judged against the metric named in step 0. Clipping is a trade against the tariff shape, not an error.
  8. Loss ladder and sanity. Every loss present even if approximate, soiling scrutinised hardest in dusty climates, and the top-down check: site irradiation times a plausible PR should land in the same postcode as the simulation.
  9. Uncertainty. P50 is the median, P90 the year exceeded nine times in ten, and the gap is set by how honest the meteo and modelling uncertainties are. The design lives at P50; lenders size debt at P90.
  10. What preliminary design actually delivers. The layout, the yield, the single-line concept, and the assumptions register: assumed versus confirmed, so the design carries an honest statement of what it is contingent on.

The string design consequence: Voc is highest on the coldest, brightest morning of the year, and that value multiplied up the string is what must stay under the inverter’s voltage ceiling. Vmp is lowest on the hottest afternoon, and that sets whether the string still sits inside the MPPT window. Every string calculation is really these two curves evaluated at the site’s two worst days.

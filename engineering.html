Worked example When the measurement plan fails — wind data without the wind ▸

The grid was for a Christmas tree farm near Bristol, built around an old American-style wind pump being converted into a turbine. The plan for the wind resource was empirical: fit a Weibull distribution to the local weather-station data, install an anemometer on the farm, and use the difference between the two to shift the distribution to what the turbine, surrounded by trees and far lower than the station’s mast, would actually see.

The plan failed in the field. During the measurement window the wind simply did not show up — most readings fell below the anemometer’s 0.7 m/s threshold and registered as zero, leaving no usable correction factor and no time to rerun the campaign. The replacement was analytical: the wind shear power law, V = V₁₀(H/10)1/α, with the exponent set by the ground surface, which for that site’s geography reduced the station data by 12% at the wind pump’s height. From there, the kinetic energy passing through the blades’ swept area — an annulus, since the blades start well clear of the hub — gave an estimated power curve, which was matched to a commercial turbine with a similar characteristic and interpolated at every time-stamped wind speed in the dataset.

The report is explicit that this is the study’s main limitation: an assumption-built power curve is not the same thing as measured performance. The exercise became less about finding a perfect answer and more about making defensible engineering assumptions with incomplete information — which, in practice, is most of the job.

Worked example How long does a battery actually last — and what is that worth? ▸

The battery question was my section, and it is mostly an economics question wearing electrical clothes: batteries are the most expensive part of a microgrid, so the design lives or dies on getting two things right — how much usable capacity the site’s climate leaves you, and how hard you are allowed to cycle the battery before the lifetime maths turns against you.

The first is temperature. Capacity falls away sharply in the cold and plateaus once it is warm; reading the datasheet curve at the Amazon site’s 28°C average actually puts capacity slightly above nominal, at 100.2%. That is a rare case where the environment helps rather than hurts, and it means no derating penalty had to be carried through the rest of the sizing.

The second is depth of discharge, and it is the more interesting trade. Cycling shallow makes a battery last far longer, so the cumulative energy it delivers over its whole life — folding the cycle-life curve into the rated capacity and the temperature correction — actually peaks around a 53% depth of discharge, not at 100%. The honest optimum is to barely use the battery and replace it rarely. But reaching the community’s demand at 53% would need a far larger, more expensive bank, so the design deliberately overruled its own optimum and took 80%: you give up a slice of lifetime energy to halve the up-front capital. Naming that trade explicitly — rather than quietly reporting the number you picked — was the part I was most pleased with.

Calc note Busbar pairing as a combinatorics problem ▸

Each row in a data hall is fed by a pair of overhead busbar strings, each loaded to 50% so that either can carry the full row if the other fails. The strings arrive from two sides of the hall, each side fed from its own MV ring. The question is which pair feeds which row.

Treat it as combinatorics. Take an illustrative hall with eight strings, four per side: eight strings give 28 unique pairs, and the rows must draw their pairs from that set under two constraints. First, balance — every string should appear in the same number of rows, or some strings idle while others carry the building. Second, side-mixing — a pair drawn from one side only shares a single upstream ring, so one ring outage silently removes both halves of that row's supposedly redundant supply. Cross-side pairs survive the loss of an entire ring; same-side pairs only pretend to.

I built an algorithm that generates the full combination set for any string count, then selects and orders an assignment that balances string utilisation and maximises cross-side pairing. The practical value was speed under change: when the string count or rack rating moved — and on a live job it moves — the assignment regenerated in seconds rather than being re-derived by hand.

First stop. New York is everything you expect and nothing like you imagined -- Times Square, the Empire State, a rally outside Trump Tower, Central Park, Brooklyn and the view back. Washington for the monuments and Arlington Cemetery. Miami for the beach, Wynwood, and the clothes.

Region 02

Southeast Asia

Thailand · Vietnam · Cambodia · Indonesia · Bali

The region that probably changes you most the first time. The density of life, the heat, the noise -- and the contrast between the tourist trail and everything just off it. You realise quickly how much of the world runs on very little, and how much that does not stop people.

Region 03

Australia

Sydney · Melbourne · East Coast

Four months living and working here, including a multi-level Melbourne venue. Cool, sunny, and the place that taught me most about integrating quickly into a team where you know nobody. The east coast is beautiful in a way that takes you by surprise.

Region 04

Japan

Tokyo · Kyoto · Osaka

Sleek and ancient at the same time. Tokyo moves faster than anywhere else on earth and Kyoto feels like it is from a different century entirely. A place that rewards paying attention -- and one that feels like two completely different countries depending on where you are standing.

Africa

Morocco · Marrakech · the Atlas Mountains

Hot, vast, and humbling. The kind of place that makes you very aware of how little of the world you actually know. The scale of everything -- the landscape, the wildlife, the contrast between what people have and what they build with it -- stays with you.

Europe

UK · France · Spain · Italy · and more

The familiar made unfamiliar. Easier to travel, harder to pay attention. But different cities have different pulses -- and noticing that, the way a place moves and what people care about, is what keeps travel interesting long after the novelty wears off.

South America

Mountains, rainforest, colour and a pace of life that makes you question why you were ever in a rush. The continent where the university microgrid project stopped being abstract -- communities without reliable power are not a case study when you have spent time in them.

More than a gap year

What it actually did

Two years travelling gave me a real appreciation for how differently people live -- the varying standards of living, the cultural contrasts, and the challenges communities face. I was also struck by how little people, me included, need to be content as long as their needs are met; that standards always rise with lifestyle. It started to make me wonder whether all of this, the extravagance we come to expect in the west, is really necessary.

Seeing the struggles of many communities, and the beauty of the world, also left me with a genuine drive to do work that has impact beyond a project deliverable, and pointed me towards meaningful engineering as the direction I want my career to go.

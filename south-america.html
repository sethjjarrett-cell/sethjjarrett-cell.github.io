Region 07

South America

Colombia, Peru, Bolivia. Three months and probably the best of it. Mountains, rainforest, salt flats, silver mines, dog shelters in the Sacred Valley, dynamite in Bolivia, and the kind of hostels that make you wonder why you ever go home.

Stop 01

Colombia

My favourite country on the continent. The north -- the Sierra Nevada, the hostels, the biodiversity, the people -- was probably the best period of the whole two years. I look back and it was just unbelievable.

Sierra Nevada
Tayrona
Valle de Cocora

I started in Bogota -- free walking tours, the graffiti, the clubs. There is a techno club, and a venue that claims to be the biggest gay club in Latin America. It is enormous. Then Medellin, which I found slightly overrun with travellers by the end -- the Thursday comunita was the highlight there. I went out to Valle de Cocora from Medellin, a coffee plantation area with wax palm trees and mountain hikes. I needed the break from the city by that point.

Then north, which was a different world. The Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta is the second largest coastal mountain range in the world and one of the most biodiverse places on earth. The climate shifts completely as you climb. The hostels up there -- Minka, Castilloma, Puerto Alto, Journey -- were the best I came across in all my travels. Minka had a DJ set at sunset over the mountains that was one of the finest things I saw anywhere. I had a rough time personally during that period, but looking back it was the best of it. Tayrona National Park, Palomino, then Cartagena to finish -- colonial architecture, good nights out, reggaeton bars.

Bogota
First stop. Walking tours, graffiti, big clubs. A real city with a lot going on.
Medellin
Good hostels, good energy, but it gets bubble-like quickly. The Thursday comunita was the best evening there.
Valle de Cocora
A day out from Medellin. Coffee farms, wax palms, mountain walks. The landscape genuinely does not look real.
Sierra Nevada & the north
The highlight of the whole trip. Second largest coastal mountain range in the world. Minka, Castilloma, Puerto Alto, Journey hostel -- the best places I stayed anywhere.
Palomino
A laid-back beach town on the coast. A few days to decompress between the mountains and Tayrona.
Tayrona National Park
Jungle and beaches in the same place. We rushed it, which I regret. People spotted jaguars on the trails.
Cartagena
Spanish colonial city on the Caribbean. Good nights out. The reggaeton bars were exactly what they should be.

Stop 02

Peru

Peru has more range than I expected. The Sacred Valley was the heart of it -- the dog retreat centre, Pisac, the people I met there. I ended up staying longer than planned, which was the right call.

Huacachina
Sacred Valley
Pisac ruins

I came in through Lima -- good food, not much else -- then got the bus down to Huacachina, which is a genuine oasis in the middle of the desert. Sandboarding for about twenty quid, a surprisingly good party scene. Strange and unexpectedly fun.

The Sacred Valley dog retreat centre via Workaway was around six pounds a day with food included. I spent about a week there -- one of the highlights of the entire trip. Beautiful location, good community, and a good base for everything around it. I walked up through the Pisac ruins one day, which was one of the better days I had anywhere. Cycled the valley, fell off the bike and shredded my legs. Went to Ollantaytambo, which was much quieter than Machu Picchu and a better walk. Did Machu Picchu via the Salkantay trek with a group. Worth doing it the long way.

Lima
Stopped for the food. Good food. Not much else to report.
Huacachina
Desert oasis. Went sandboarding. Good party scene I wasn't expecting. Strange place in the best way.
Sacred Valley dog retreat
Around £6/day with food via Workaway. Stayed about a week. One of the highlights of the two years.
Pisac
Hippie town with Inca ruins above it. Walked up through the ruins. One of the better days of the trip.
Ollantaytambo
Quieter than Machu Picchu, better walk, almost no other tourists. Felt like a proper find.
Machu Picchu
Did it via the Salkantay trek with a group. Worth the extra effort over the bus route.

Stop 03

Bolivia

The best value country I have ever been to, and probably the most varied three weeks of the whole trip. Huayna Potosi was the standout -- possibly the best thing I did in two years of travelling.

Huayna Potosi
Salt flats
La Paz

I started in La Paz -- built into a canyon at over 3,600 metres, with cable cars running across it and views that stop you. I went to one of the coca bars, which was interesting in the sense that nothing enjoyable happened and I did not sleep. Then Huayna Potosi: three days, crampons, a 3am summit push at 6,088 metres. Under a hundred pounds all-in including food, accommodation, and all gear. The hardest thing I did on the trip, and probably the most worthwhile.

Potosi was sobering in a different way. Twenty pounds to go into the silver mines that funded the Spanish empire for three centuries -- the conditions are still brutal, the miners still young. I spoke to some of them. I bought dynamite from a street stall outside and, with the guide, used it in the mountain. I lit it, had no idea where to put it down, eventually just left it and ran. Then it went off. Sucre was a good few days afterwards -- quiet, warm, beautiful colonial streets. I did ayahuasca in Bolivia, which was not the most traditional setting but was still a significant experience. The salt flats were a day trip -- white to every horizon, one of the great landscapes, and one day felt like enough.

La Paz
Built into a canyon at 3,600m. Cable cars, stunning views, witches market. Went to a coca bar. Interesting, not enjoyable.
Huayna Potosi
6,088 metres. Three days, crampons, summit at 3am. Under £100 all-in. Probably the best thing I did in two years.
Potosi mines
Twenty pounds to go in. Spoke to the miners. Bought dynamite on the street, used it in the mountain. The whole thing was slightly mad.
Sucre
Bolivia's constitutional capital. White colonial city, warm, quiet. A good few days after Potosi.
Ayahuasca
Did it in Bolivia. Not the most traditional setting, but a significant experience nonetheless.
Salar de Uyuni
The salt flats. White to every horizon. Did a day trip -- one day felt right.

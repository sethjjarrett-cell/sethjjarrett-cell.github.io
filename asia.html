Region 02

South & Southeast Asia

Nepal, Cambodia, Thailand, Laos, Vietnam, Malaysia, Singapore, Indonesia, Philippines. Nine countries across about six months -- my first stint outside the West. The first real culture shock in Kathmandu, the Ha Giang Loop, paramotoring, the Full Moon Party for New Year, bioluminescent waves on Koh Rong, Muay Thai, the killing fields, Angkor Wat at sunrise, and a boxing match at a reggae bar on Koh Phi Phi.

Stop 01

Nepal

The first stop out of the West, and a proper culture shock. Landing in Kathmandu and getting straight into a taxi -- no seatbelt to be seen, the driver swerving through traffic while on the phone -- set the tone immediately. Then Pokhara, Phewa Lake, and the Himalayas at a scale nothing prepares you for.

From the notebook

Walking through Thamel, it was the smell that overwhelmed me -- fumes, food, dirt, sewage, incense and sweat, all at once. Too much for my brain to process.

Kathmandu is chaotic and clearly poor -- a city of temples, dust and traffic. I climbed up to the Monkey Temple (Swayambhunath), saw monkeys for the first time and got a great view across the city. Got too close to a mother and her baby and very nearly paid for it. The food was great, heavily Indian-inspired.

Then the coach to Pokhara, treacherous after a recent storm -- they had cleared landslides that morning and the driver picked his way along a rock-strewn road. Worth it: the lake, the stupa across the water, the Fishtail Mountain viewpoint at sunrise. Annapurna stands at over 8,000m; from the 800m of Pokhara, it is genuinely incomprehensible.

Kathmandu
Hectic, colourful, historic. The Monkey Temple. Genuinely disorienting as a first stop -- the kind of place that hits you all at once.
Pokhara
The lake, the stupa at sunrise, Fishtail Mountain. Calmer than Kathmandu. The Himalayas right there.

Stop 02

Cambodia

A country that wears its history visibly. The genocide museum and the killing fields in Phnom Penh are harrowing -- a five-metre stack of real skulls is an experience that stays with you. Then the coast, the islands, and Angkor Wat, which earns its reputation.

From the notebook

In Kep national park we visited a hotel -- ginormous, dilapidated, car park empty, nobody inside but the staff. Eerie, like a set from American Horror Story. The guide mentioned an abandoned laboratory further up, still partly in use, so off limits. Who knows what happened up there. A microcosm of Cambodia's history.

Kampot and Kep on the southern coast are sleepy and a little strange. The national park near Kep has abandoned buildings half-swallowed by jungle -- lots of them half-finished, unsettling, and tied to the Khmer Rouge years in ways that make you uneasy rather than curious.

Koh Rong and Koh Rong Samloem are some of the best islands in Southeast Asia, and probably -- likely always -- the highlight of my entire travels. Arriving somewhere that beautiful for the first time, free of home, surrounded by like-minded travellers your own age: the euphoria is unbelievable. I think that feeling has ruined my life slightly. I am not sure I will ever fully capture it again, and I find myself looking back to moments like it when times are tough at home. You can use places like that to escape, but it is never permanent.

There is bioluminescence in the waves at night on Koh Rong, so the sea glows as it laps the shore. It made the place feel surreal -- a paradise island with glowing water, just off a country still carrying the weight of its recent history. It felt a bit wrong to be there having such a good time. Westerners are strange people: incredibly sheltered, and able to detach from the reality of things with worrying ease.

Then Angkor Wat at Siem Reap, a standard stop -- and it surprised me. Sometimes the iconic things are iconic for a reason. Wandering the temples feels like walking into Tomb Raider.

Phnom Penh
The genocide museum and the killing fields. Heavy. The kind of place that stays with you.
Kampot and Kep
Sleepy coastal towns. Good seafood. Kep national park has abandoned Khmer Rouge-era buildings that are genuinely unsettling.
Koh Rong and Koh Rong Samloem
The islands. Bioluminescent waves at night -- the sea genuinely glows. One of the better things on the whole trip.
Angkor Wat
A standard stop for a reason -- I went at sunrise. Looks exactly like Tomb Raider.

Stop 03

Thailand

Visited twice -- north first with Bangkok, Pai and Chiang Mai, then the islands on the way back through. Christmas on Koh Tao (lost a phone and a chunk of my ear; all is well though), New Year at the Full Moon Party on Koh Phangan, a Muay Thai camp on Koh Yao Yai, and a boxing match at a reggae bar on Koh Phi Phi. Ko Lipe was the best island of the whole trip -- the best of the Thai islands without the crowds.

From the notebook

The boxing match at the reggae bar on Phi Phi: serious tunnel vision, pure adrenaline. Won, against my friend, and left pretty dazed. We were given a bucket of Long Island iced tea and the rest of the evening is hazy. Probably not medically advisable.

Pai is a small hippie town in the mountains north of Chiang Mai, definitely worth stopping for -- even a few days' break. I probably stayed too long and got a bit antsy toward the end. The canyon nearby is good, and Muay Thai training in Chiang Mai is worthwhile even if you come out of it with sore ribs and a sorer ego.

The camp on Koh Yao Yai is where martial arts kicked off properly for me. Phi Phi had "The Beach" from The Beach -- probably the most picturesque beach I have been to, and tourism has clearly ruined it.

Ko Lipe, near the Malaysian border, has a different quality to the more visited islands: quieter, good food, just well-travelled enough to have proper hostels and restaurants without the loud salesmen.

Bangkok
Enormous, chaotic, worth a night or two each time through.
Chiang Mai and Pai
Northern Thailand. Muay Thai training, temples, mountains. Pai is small and genuinely good.
Koh Tao
Christmas here. Lost a phone and part of an ear. Both resolved.
Full Moon Party, Koh Phangan
New Year. Once is enough and worth it.
Koh Yao Yai
Muay Thai camp. Private lessons. The kicks started making sense here.
Koh Phi Phi
Boxing match at a reggae bar. Tunnel vision, adrenaline, won. One of the stranger evenings.
Ko Lipe
Favourite island in all of Southeast Asia. Near the Malaysian border, quieter, genuinely beautiful.

Stop 04

Laos

A small, quiet country that got bombed harder than anywhere in history -- more ordnance dropped on Laos than in the entirety of World War I and II combined, targeting the Ho Chi Minh Trail. That context matters when you're there. Nong Khiaw down the river, Vang Vieng for the chaos, Luang Prabang, and a capital that is famously the most boring in the world.

Nong Khiaw sits between karst mountains on the Nam Ou river -- got there by boat, met locals, genuinely beautiful and almost entirely untouristy. Vang Vieng is the opposite: motorbiking, caving, and a party scene that is properly hectic. Nearly collided on the bikes at speed, which was one of the more frightening moments of the whole trip.

Vientiane, the capital, has almost nothing going on -- supposedly the quietest capital city in the world. I arrived expecting something and there was nothing. That is accurate.

Nong Khiaw
Down the river by boat. Karst mountains, locals, almost no tourists. One of the best stops.
Vang Vieng
Motorbiking, caving, tubing, a mad party scene. Nearly crashed at speed. Properly wild.
Luang Prabang
Temples, the Mekong, monks at sunrise. I slowed down here and it was the right call.
Vientiane
The capital. Allegedly the quietest in the world. That is accurate.

Stop 05

Vietnam

Hanoi was a favourite city in all of Asia -- a proper jungle-city hybrid with the communist presence still tangible in the museums and the way history is framed. The Ha Giang Loop in the far north is the standout experience: four days on motorbikes through mountain roads, rice terraces, and mist, narrowly avoiding disaster, five crashes in the group, mine included. Worth every bit of it.

The Ha Giang Loop is four days on a semi-automatic in the mountains near the Chinese border -- no driving licences, police checkpoints navigated by walking the bike through and remounting the other side, nights drinking in remote villages, cloud and mist for most of it. Five people in the group crashed. Wing mirrors lost. Nobody found. That is partly the appeal.

Ha Long Bay on a boat with the group from the loop was one of the best single days of the whole trip. Heading south: Hue for the history, the Hai Van Pass by motorbike (do it), Hoi An for the lanterns on the river at night -- genuinely beautiful -- then Ho Chi Minh City, which is loud and fast and feels almost retro in a way Hanoi doesn't.

Hanoi
Favourite city in Southeast Asia. Jungle-city hybrid, communist history visible everywhere.
Ha Giang Loop
Four days on a motorbike in the far north. Five crashes, one wing mirror gone, no licences, Chinese border. One of the best things I did.
Ha Long Bay
A day on a boat with the loop group. One of the best days of the whole trip.
Hue and the Hai Van Pass
Did the pass by motorbike. Hue had good history and good food.
Hoi An
Lanterns on the river at night. Genuinely beautiful. Stayed a couple of days.
Ho Chi Minh City
The south. Loud, fast, hectic, slightly retro. The war museums are uncomfortable -- that is the point.

Stop 06

Malaysia

Hot, with exceptional food. Langkawi for the islands, Georgetown for the heritage streets, Kuala Lumpur as a genuinely huge city that feels faintly cyberpunk and slightly grimy in parts. Not the most exciting country on the route but the food is some of the best in the region.

Langkawi is very hot and very good for food -- good place to slow down coming off Vietnam. Georgetown has beautiful heritage streets but the city around it is not particularly appealing. Kuala Lumpur is enormous -- genuinely massive, a bit dirty in places. I spent a night or two there.

Langkawi
Very hot. Good food, good islands. A decent place to decompress.
Georgetown, Penang
Beautiful heritage streets, good food. The wider city is not the draw.
Kuala Lumpur
Huge, slightly cyberpunk, a bit rough in places. Spent a night or two there. Petronas Towers.

Stop 07

Singapore

The cleanest, most expensive city in the region. Completely unlike anywhere else in Southeast Asia -- a city-state that has engineered itself into something singular. The drink at Marina Bay Sands with a friend, looking out over the skyline, was one of those evenings that is difficult to top.

Spent a couple of days between Malaysia and Indonesia. Expensive, spotlessly clean, fascinating in the way that very deliberate cities are. The view from the Marina Bay Sands rooftop was as good as advertised.

Marina Bay Sands
Had a drink on the rooftop with a friend. One of the better views I saw in Asia.
The hawker centres
Cheap, excellent food everywhere. Ate at as many as I could.

Stop 08

Philippines

Visited on the way back from Australia. Some of the best beaches anywhere -- warm, clear, genuinely beautiful. The culture felt more authentic and warm than much of Southeast Asia. Manila is heaving and dirty and worth seeing for exactly that reason.

The beaches are among the best in the world -- the water is the right colour and the culture around them feels genuine rather than performed for tourists. Manila is one of the most chaotic cities in the region, a proper megacity that is not trying to be anything else. Worth a day.

Manila
Dirty, heaving, chaotic. A proper megacity. Spent a day there rather than skipping it.
The beaches
Some of the best in the world. The culture around them is warm and feels real.

Stop 09

Indonesia

Bali with a now-ex -- explored the island properly on motorbikes, rode down into the Kintamani caldera and saw the volcanic landscape from inside the basin. Jakarta is the least favourite city of the whole trip: dangerous roads, nothing particularly appealing, not recommended.

Explored Bali on a motorbike -- rode out to Kintamani, dropped into the volcanic caldera and looked back up at the rim. The geological scale is impressive. Jakarta I would not go back to.

Bali
Explored by motorbike. Rode to Kintamani and down into the caldera -- one of the better days on the island.
Jakarta
Least favourite city of the whole trip. Dangerous roads, nothing that pulled me in.
